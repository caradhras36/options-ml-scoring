# I trained an XGBoost pipeline on 363K simulated options trades — here's what actually worked (and what didn't)

Most options traders selling premium rely on a handful of heuristics: *sell at 30 delta, close at 50% profit, avoid earnings.* These work well enough — until they don't.

I wanted to see if a model could do better than heuristics at answering one question: **given the current IV environment, technical setup, and earnings calendar, will this specific short option reach 50% profit before expiry?**

This post is a walkthrough of the pipeline I built, the results I got, and — importantly — why you should be skeptical of the numbers. I'll show the SHAP explanations alongside each section so you can see what the model actually learned vs. what might be noise.

> **Caveat up front:** This is trained on simulated trades using EOD mid-prices, not real executions. No slippage, no commissions, no bid-ask spread. The backtest numbers are directionally interesting but not a P&L you'd actually realize. I'm sharing this for the methodology, not as trading advice.

---

## The Data: 1.73 Million Simulated Trades

Real options trade data at scale is hard to come by. So I built my own simulation on top of **OptionsDX historical EOD chains (2020–2023)** for six underlyings: AAPL, NVDA, QQQ, SPX, SPY, TSLA.

For every trading day in the dataset, I identified every option meeting these criteria:
- 21–60 DTE
- |Delta| between 0.10 and 0.40
- Mid price > $0.05

Then I simulated each trade: open a short position at mid price, track the option's mid price daily, and record whether it hit 50% profit, how long it took, the final P&L at expiry, and the outcome category (full win / partial win / breakeven / loss).

The raw OptionsDX dataset has ~1.73M rows (every strike × every day × call/put). After filtering, that drops sharply:

```
1.73M  raw option chain rows (every strike/day/expiry combo)
  → candidate filter: DTE 21-60, |delta| 0.10-0.40, mid > $0.05
  → data quality: IV 5-300%, sane gamma/theta/vega bounds
  → simulation: open short at mid, track daily to expiry
  → feature engineering: RSI, Bollinger, IVR need 252-day rolling windows
     (first ~year of each ticker is NaN → dropped)
363K  final training rows
```

So the 363K number is what the model actually trained on. The 1.73M is how many raw chain rows went into the pipeline — most get filtered because they're ATM, too short/long DTE, or lack enough history for the rolling indicators.

**Why this is imperfect:** Mid-price execution is optimistic. In practice you'd fill closer to the natural side (bid for sells, ask for buys). On liquid names like SPY this matters less; on something like TSLA weeklies it matters a lot. The simulation also doesn't account for early assignment risk or margin requirements.

## The Features: 19 Signals Per Trade

Each candidate gets 19 features in four groups:

### Option Characteristics (10)

| Feature | What It Captures |
|---------|-----------------|
| DTE | Time to expiration — theta accelerates under 21 days |
| Delta | Probability proxy — lower delta = farther OTM |
| Gamma | Rate of delta change — high gamma near expiry = assignment risk |
| Theta | Daily time decay in dollars |
| Vega | IV sensitivity |
| IV | Implied volatility of this specific contract |
| Moneyness | Strike / stock price ratio |
| Annualized Return | Premium / capital-at-risk, annualized |
| Credit Received | Dollar premium per contract |
| Max Loss | Worst-case scenario |

### Technical Indicators (4)

RSI-14, MACD signal (+1/−1/0), Bollinger %B, and a resistance score measuring how close the strike is to 90-day high/low pivots. Computed from the underlying's price history.

### Volatility Regime (2)

**IV Rank** (where IV sits vs. 52-week range) and **IV Percentile** (% of days with lower IV). These turned out to be some of the most important features — more on that below.

### Earnings Context (3)

Days to earnings, earnings-in-window (binary), post-earnings (binary).

**A lesson learned the hard way:** I initially used `999` as a sentinel for "no earnings" (ETFs like SPY). XGBoost treated this as a real value and learned nonsensical splits. Switching to `NaN` — letting XGBoost handle missingness natively — was the **single biggest model improvement**: +2pp AUC and +14pp R² on the EV model. Don't encode "missing" as a magic number.

## The Models: 5 XGBoost Ensembles

I train five models on the same 19 features, each answering a different question:

### Model 1: Will it hit 50%? (Binary Classifier)

The primary model. At trade entry, what's the probability of reaching 50% profit?

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| AUC-ROC | 0.982 |
| Precision (overall) | 99.1% |
| Recall | 91.6% |
| F1 | 95.2% |

At the 0.85 confidence threshold: precision rises to 99.95%, but recall drops to 47.6%. The model becomes extremely selective — it only flags ~143K of 300K candidates, but when it says "yes," it's almost always right. The trade-off is it passes on more than half the winners.

**Why precision is misleadingly high:** The base rate is 88% — most short options in this delta range expire profitably anyway. The model needs to be compared against a "sell everything" baseline, not zero. I'll show that comparison in the backtest section.

### Model 2: Max Profit at Expiry (Regressor)
- MAE: 0.47 (on a 0–1+ scale) — R²: 0.70

### Model 3: Days to 50% (Regressor)
- MAE: 2.8 days — R²: 0.63
- Only trained on trades that actually hit 50%. Median in data: 6 days.

### Model 4: Expected Dollar Value (Regressor)
- MAE: $596 — R²: 0.87

### Model 5: Outcome Category (Multi-class)
- Accuracy: 82.7% — Loss recall: ~80%
- Classes: full_win (≥80%), partial_win (20–80%), breakeven (±20%), loss (<−20%)
- Class imbalance handled with inverse-frequency sample weights.

## What the Model Actually Learned: SHAP Deep Dive

This is the part I find most interesting. Instead of trusting the accuracy numbers, let's look at *what features drive predictions* using TreeSHAP.

### A trade the model liked: QQQ $308 Call, Oct 2022

```
Underlying: $280.08  |  Moneyness: 1.10  |  Delta: +0.126  |  29 DTE
Actual: hit 50% ✓  |  Max profit: +99.6%  |  EV: $133
Model prediction: 72.8% probability

Top SHAP drivers:
  RSI-14 = 32          ✅ +0.646  ██████   (oversold → mean reversion likely)
  Moneyness = 1.10     ❌ −0.320  ███      (far OTM → less certain)
  Delta = 0.126        ✅ +0.187  █        (low delta → high POP)
  IVR = 36             ✅ +0.176  █        (elevated IV → fatter premium)
  Resistance score = 0  ❌ −0.109  █        (no nearby resistance)
```

The model sees an oversold stock (RSI 32) with elevated IV and a far-OTM strike. It's moderately confident. The negative SHAP from moneyness says "this is pretty far out" — there's tension between the favorable setup and the distance.

### A trade the model rejected: SPX $3890 Call, Apr 2021

```
Underlying: $3769.66  |  Moneyness: 1.032  |  Delta: +0.303  |  36 DTE
Actual: loss  |  Max profit: −300%  |  EV: −$12,268
Model prediction: 11.5% probability

Top SHAP drivers:
  Delta = 0.303        ❌ −0.879  ████████  (too close to ATM)
  RSI-14 = 40          ❌ −0.613  ██████    (not oversold enough)
  Moneyness = 1.032    ❌ −0.604  ██████    (barely OTM)
  IVR = 17             ❌ −0.170  █         (low IV → thin premium)
```

This is a 0.30 delta SPX call in a low-IV environment. The model sees a near-ATM strike with inadequate premium cushion. Every major feature pushes the prediction down. This trade lost $12K — the model would have avoided it.

### An interesting edge case: SPX $4080 Call, Apr 2021

```
Underlying: $3974.80  |  Moneyness: 1.026  |  Delta: +0.303  |  44 DTE
Actual: hit 50% ✓ (but then reversed → final outcome: loss, −167%)
Model prediction: 47.3% — basically a coin flip

Top SHAP drivers:
  Delta = 0.303        ❌ −0.510  █████   (too close)
  RSI-14 = 61          ✅ +0.469  ████    (mildly bullish)
  Max loss = $408K     ✅ +0.459  ████    (high notional → model artifact?)
  Moneyness = 1.026    ❌ −0.428  ████    (barely OTM)
  IVR = 10             ❌ −0.268  ██      (very low IV)
```

This trade *did* hit 50% profit, but then reversed and expired at −167% of credit. The model's 47% score correctly reflects the ambiguity — it's not confident either way. The `max_loss` feature pushing positive here is suspicious and might be a data artifact worth investigating.

### Global Feature Importance

From the SHAP bar chart (mean |SHAP| across all predictions):

1. **Annualized return** — how rich is the premium relative to capital at risk
2. **Moneyness** — how far OTM the strike is
3. **Max loss** — the notional risk (note: this correlates with underlying price, so it may partly proxy for "which ticker")
4. **Delta** — the probability proxy
5. **IV Rank** — where IV sits historically
6. **RSI-14** — surprisingly important; the model learned mean-reversion effects

**What's interesting:** Earnings features rank low in global importance, but have high local impact when earnings actually fall in the window. The model learned that earnings *don't usually matter* (most trades don't span an earnings date), but *when they do*, they matter a lot.

## Backtest: 2022–2023 Held-Out Period

I backtested on **2 years of held-out data** (2022–2023), completely excluded from training:

| Metric | Model Picks (0.85 threshold) | Baseline (All Candidates) |
|--------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Trades | 11,543 | 28,398 |
| Hit Rate | 99.9% | 87.0% |
| Avg P&L / Trade | $734 | $468 |
| Total P&L | $8.48M | $13.28M |
| Return on Credit | 93.1% | 65.0% |
| **Lift vs Baseline** | **+57% per trade** | — |

The model takes fewer trades but makes more per trade. The 57% lift is the claimed alpha.

**Why you should be skeptical of these numbers:**

1. **Mid-price execution.** Real fills would be worse. On SPX options the spread can be $1–5, which eats directly into your premium on a $4 credit.

2. **No position sizing or margin.** The "Total P&L" number assumes you could take all 11K trades simultaneously with unlimited capital. In reality you'd have concentration limits, margin requirements, and correlation risk (selling puts on 6 correlated tickers during a drawdown is… not diversified).

3. **Survivorship in the ticker universe.** AAPL, NVDA, SPY, QQQ — these are the most liquid, most mean-reverting names. The model hasn't seen a stock that gaps 40% on earnings (think: SNAP, META in 2022) because those aren't in the training set.

4. **2022–2023 is a specific regime.** 2022 was a bear market with elevated IV (great for sellers), 2023 was a steady grind up (also great for sellers). The model hasn't been tested through a genuine low-vol environment where premiums thin out and selling becomes less profitable across the board.

5. **The base rate is 87%.** The model is improving on an already-high win rate. The real question is whether the 57% lift per trade survives transaction costs and realistic execution.

## Threshold Tuning

The 0.85 threshold is a balance between selectivity and volume:

| Threshold | Trades | Precision | Recall | Avg EV/Trade |
|-----------|--------|-----------|--------|--------------|
| 0.50 | 258K | 98.9% | 85.0% | $1,613 |
| **0.85** | **143K** | **99.95%** | **47.6%** | **$2,191** |
| 0.95 | 70K | 99.99% | 23.4% | $2,646 |
| 0.99 | 15K | 100.0% | 5.1% | $3,464 |

The precision/recall trade-off is steep. At 0.99 you're selecting 5% of candidates with (backtested) perfect precision — but you're passing on 95% of trades that would also have been winners. Whether that selectivity is worth it depends on your capital constraints and risk appetite.

## Known Limitations

**Narrow universe.** Six tickers. The features are ticker-agnostic (delta, IV, moneyness don't care about the symbol), but the model hasn't seen small-cap or biotech behavior patterns.

**No regime detection.** Trained across COVID crash, 2021 melt-up, and 2022 bear — it learned from all of them but doesn't know which one we're in now.

**VIX excluded.** VIX options had extreme moneyness values (up to 9.4x) that distorted the feature space.

**IV cold start.** IVR/IVP require 252 days of history. New tickers start with null values.

**`annualized_return` as top feature.** This is derived from the premium, which is closely related to the target. It's not target leakage in the strict sense (it's an input you know at trade entry), but the model may be over-indexing on "the premium was rich" rather than learning deeper structure. This is an area I'm still investigating.
